Abstract One of the important sources of hot ions in the magnetotail is the bursty bulk ﬂows
propagating away from the reconnection region and heating the ambient plasma. Charged particles
interact with nonlinear magnetic ﬁeld pulses (dipolarization fronts, DFs) embedded into these ﬂows. The
convection electric ﬁelds associated with DF propagation are known to reﬂect and accelerate ambient
ions. Moreover, a wide range of waves is observed within/near these fronts, the electric ﬁeld ﬂuctuations
being dominated by the lower hybrid drift (LHD) instability. Here we investigate the potential role of these
waves in the further acceleration of ambient ions. We use a LHD wave emission proﬁle superimposed on
the leading edge of a two-dimensional model proﬁle of a DF and a test particle approach. We show that
LHD waves with realistic amplitudes can signiﬁcantly increase the upper limit of energies gained by ions.
Wave-particle interaction near the front is more eﬀective in producing superthermal ions than in increasing
the ﬂux of thermal ions. Comparison of test particle simulations and Time History of Events and Macroscale
Interactions during Substorms observations show that ion acceleration by LHD waves is more important for
slower DFs.
1. Introduction
Spatiallylocalizedandtransientmagneticreconnectioninthehotplasmaofplanetarymagnetotails[Kronberg
et al., 2005; Angelopoulos et al., 2008a; Slavin et al., 2009; Kasahara et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2012; Paschmann
et al., 2013; Harada et al., 2015] transfers a signiﬁcant portion of magnetic energy into fast plasma ﬂows
[Angelopoulosetal., 1992, 2013] carrying dipolarization fronts [Nakamuraetal., 2002; Runovetal., 2009; Hwang
etal., 2011; Fuetal., 2013]. Possible mechanisms responsible for the transformation of plasma ﬂow energy into
heat have been the focus of many investigations during the last decade. Particle drifts along a dipolarization
front (DF) and the associated strong convection electric ﬁeld, which are enabled by strong magnetic ﬁeld gra-
dients, are believed to provide the signiﬁcant heating of the ambient plasma [Birn et al., 2004; Ashour-Abdalla
etal., 2011; Birnetal., 2012; Fuetal., 2012b]. These adiabatic eﬀects play a critical role for particle injections into
the inner magnetosphere [Gabrielse et al., 2012; Ganushkina et al., 2013; Gabrielse et al., 2014; Liu et al., 2016;
Turner et al., 2016]. Nonadiabatic eﬀects at DFs are also signiﬁcant for ion energization, in particular ion reﬂec-
tion [Zhou et al., 2010; Drake et al., 2014], which resembles ion reﬂection from shock waves [e.g., Gedalin, 1996;
Lee et al., 1996] and thus includes both nonresonant [Zhou et al., 2011; Greco et al., 2014; Eastwood et al., 2015]
and resonant [Artemyev et al., 2012; Ukhorskiy et al., 2013, 2017] accelerations. Adiabaticity can be destroyed
near the DF not only due to strong magnetic gradients but also due to the eﬀective particle scattering by a
broad range of electromagnetic and electrostatic wave emissions, including ion-scale ﬂuctuations [Ono et al.,
2009; Nosé et al., 2012], magnetosonic waves [M. Zhou et al., 2014], lower hybrid waves [Sergeev et al., 2009;
Khotyaintsev et al., 2011], whistler waves [Le Contel et al., 2009; Deng et al., 2010; Breuillard et al., 2016], and
electron cyclotron waves [Zhang and Angelopoulos, 2014; Zhang et al., 2015].
Models describing adiabatic electron acceleration by DFs reproduce observed electron spectra rather well
[Apatenkov et al., 2007; Fu et al., 2011; Pan et al., 2012; Gabrielse et al., 2016]. Numerical simulations [Divin et al.,
2015b] and observations accompanied with modeling, have revealed the eﬀects of nonadiabaticity due to
electron scattering by waves for various wave emissions [Panov et al., 2013; Zhang and Angelopoulos, 2014;
M. Zhou et al., 2014; Divin et al., 2015a]. However, the eﬀect of waves on ion interaction with the DF is less
clear. This eﬀect strongly depends on the front conﬁguration [Greco et al., 2014; X.-Z. Zhou et al., 2014] and its
evolution during earthward propagation [Artemyev, 2014]. In contrast to electrons, which drift along the front

this paper, we upgrade the two-dimensional (2-D) DF model proposed by Greco et al. [2014] and incorporate
LHD wave emissions into it, in accordance with observations. We use the electrostatic approximation for LHD
waves and parameterize wave characteristics using Time History of Events and Macroscale Interactions dur-
ing Substorms (THEMIS) spacecraft observations of dipolarization fronts. Finally, we compare results of ion
acceleration at DF with and without LHD waves and spacecraft observations of ion spectra. This comparison
allows us to estimate the relative importance of LHD waves for ion interaction with the DF, depending on
front parameters.

4.1. THEMIS Observations
We use spin resolution of magnetic ﬁeld measurements from the ﬂuxgate magnetometer [Auster et al., 2008]
and combined measurements from the electrostatic analyzer (ESA) [McFadden et al., 2008] and Solid State
Telescope (SST) [e.g., Angelopoulos et al., 2008b] of ion bulk velocity vx (all vectors in GSM coordinates). For
measurements of LHD wave emission we use the THEMIS Electric Field Instrument (EFI) which measures the
electric ﬁelds in three directions [Bonnell et al., 2008]. Digital Fields Board (DFB) performs the spectral pro-
cessing for the EFI on board [Cully et al., 2008]. We use the THEMIS Filter Bank (FBK) data providing a power
spectrum by averaging the absolute value of frequency range, i.e., we use the distribution of mean electric
ﬁeld amplitudes over six logarithmic frequency bins at a 3 s cadence (mean frequencies are 2689 Hz, 572 Hz,
144.2 Hz, 36.2 Hz, 9.05 Hz, and 2.26 Hz). The FBK spectra contain only in-plane electric ﬁeld. However, this lim-
itation does not inﬂuence our results, because we consider LDH waves propagating almost in electrostatic

be considered as a reasonable compromise, because the observed structure of LHD waves is rather localized
in time (and thus localized in space), whereas our model considers that LHD emission occupies the DF leading
edge almost uniformly. We do not expect any nonlinear eﬀect of wave-particle interaction (e.g., phase bunch-
ing and trapping, see Palmadesso [1972], Karimabadi et al. [1990], and Shklyar and Zimbardo [2014]) for ion
interaction with LHD waves. Therefore, we can approximate a small number of very intense wave structures
by a more uniform distribution of low-intensity waves. In this case, FBK data provides exactly the averaged
wave spectra which are required by the numerical model.
An important simpliﬁcation of our model corresponds to the 2-D equatorial approximation of the DF mag-
netic ﬁeld. Indeed, reduction of magnetic ﬁeld conﬁguration results in consideration of only equatorial
particles which cannot escape from (or arrive to) the acceleration region moving along magnetic ﬁeld lines.
Such ﬁeld-aligned accelerated ion beams (see observations and modeling in Zhou et al. [2012b] and Birn
et al. [2013]) should be subject to scattering by LHD waves around DF leading edge. However, an accurate
modeling of LHD intensity distribution in 3-D space requires a separate investigation of both LHD waves
generation/propagation [Divin et al., 2015b] and 3-D DF conﬁguration [Liu et al., 2011, 2013]. Such an investi-
gation of particle acceleration in a 3-D geometry was carried out for a plasmoid ﬁlled by magnetosonic waves
[Grigorenko et al., 2015], where the presence of electromagnetic ﬂuctuations was shown to signiﬁcantly
intensify ion energization.
To conclude, we have investigated the equatorial ion interaction with a 2-D DF carrying at its leading edge
an intense LHD wave emission. These waves propagate along the front with ion diamagnetic drift velocity
comparable with the velocity of the DF earthward propagation. Thus, ions reﬂected from the DF interact
with the LHD waves as if with coherent, stationary electrostatic ﬁeld structures. The ion interaction with such
structures can signiﬁcantly increase the ion energy gain. The relative contribution of LHD waves to ion ener-
gization becomes important for slow (or low amplitude) DFs, for which ion reﬂection from the front has low
acceleration eﬃciency. The comparison between test particle modeling and spacecraft observations conﬁrms
the importance of LHD waves for ion interaction with slow (low amplitude of convection electric ﬁeld) DFs.
Therefore, we can conclude that wave LHD generation, resulting from free energy release from unstable ion
distributions at the front, provides the energy transfer between embedded ion populations (responsible for
LHD waves growth) and transient ion population reﬂection from DF.
