Abstract This study presents a theoretical approach to calculate the inductive electric ﬁeld, and it
is further applied to global MHD simulations of the magnetosphere. The contribution of the inductive
component to the total electric ﬁeld is found by decomposing the motional electric ﬁeld into a
superposition of an irrotational and a solenoidal vector and assuming that the time-varying magnetic ﬁeld
vanishes on the boundary. We ﬁnd that a localized change in the magnetic ﬁeld generates an inductive
electric ﬁeld whose eﬀect extends over all space, meaning that the eﬀect of the inductive electric ﬁeld is
global even if the changes in the magnetic ﬁeld are localized. Application of this formalism to disturbed
times provides strong evidence that during periods of increased activity the electric ﬁeld induced by the
localized change in magnetic ﬁeld can be comparable to (or larger than) the potential electric ﬁelds in
certain regions. This induced ﬁeld exhibits signiﬁcant spatial and temporal variations, which means that
particles that drift into diﬀerent regions of space are being exposed to diﬀerent means of acceleration. These
results suggest that the inductive electric ﬁeld could have a substantial contribution to particle energization
in the near-Earth region even though the changes in the magnetic ﬁelds occur at distances of several tens
of Earth radii. This ﬁnding is particularly important for ring current modeling which in many cases excludes
inductive contributions to the total particle drift.
1. Introduction
The terrestrial magnetosphere has the capability to rapidly accelerate charged particles up to high energies
over relatively short times and distances, leading to an increase in the near-Earth currents. These energetic
particles are injected from the magnetotail into the inner magnetosphere through two primary mechanisms.
One transport method is potential-driven convection. This occurs during periods of southward interplanetary
magnetic ﬁeld (IMF), which allows part of the dawn-to-dusk solar wind electric ﬁeld to eﬀectivelymapdownto
thepolarionosphere[Dungey,1961;Volland,1973].Thesecondtransportprocess,theinductivedrift,iscaused
by the intense electric ﬁelds created from the magnetotail collapse during substorm activity, and as opposed
to the potential-driven convection, which is a large-scale phenomena, it has a localized nature. This inductive
drift is due to the sudden reconﬁguration of the magnetic ﬁeld and the creation of localized transient-induced
electric ﬁelds [Nishida et al., 1981; Baker et al., 1996].
Earthward injections of energetic particles are characteristic signatures of the expansion phase of substorms
in the near-Earth magnetotail, together with other phenomena such as rapid dipolarization of the magneto-
spheric ﬁeld lines [e.g., Sauvaud and Winckler, 1980; Ukhorskiy et al., 2013; Pan et al., 2014] or intense transient
electric ﬁelds [e.g., Aggson et al., 1983]. Small-scale particle injections that eﬀectively transport energetic ions
deep into the inner magnetosphere [e.g., Gkioulidou et al., 2014] alter the population of the trapped parti-
cles in the inner magnetosphere region and are key features for both geomagnetic storms and substorms.
This eﬃcient acceleration of charged particles and their subsequent injection into near-Earth space leads to a
large magnetic ﬁeld depression during the main phase of geomagnetic storms. To date, it is not clear whether
this depression and therefore the intensiﬁcation of the storm time ring current primarily results from the
long-lasting enhancement of the large-scale magnetospheric convection [Wodnicka, 1989; Wolf et al., 1997]
or from substorm-related plasma injections [e.g., Fok et al., 1999].
Storm dipolarization events also lead to increased inductive electric ﬁelds which signiﬁcantly alter the ring
current dynamics, leading to both rapid inward transport and prominent nonadiabatic energization of the

et al., 2002; Yang et al., 2016].
The inclusion of inductive electric ﬁelds generated by time-varying magnetic ﬁelds could play an important
role in a realistic description of the convection in numerical models. The energy of the particles can only
change by means of the electric ﬁeld; therefore, understanding the energization processes requires detailed
knowledge of the origin of the electromagnetic ﬁelds. However, the inclusion and calculation of the induc-
tive component of the electric ﬁeld in numerical models of the inner magnetosphere has been challenging
[e.g., Toivanen, 2007; Zaharia et al., 2008]. Simplistic models exist for the inductive electric ﬁelds [e.g., Li et al.,
1998; Sarris et al., 2002] and have been used in drift physics transport models to explain injections into
near-Earth space [e.g., Ganushkina et al., 2001, 2005]. Other models that included such ﬁelds [Wolf, 1983; Fok
andMoore, 1997] are based on an ionospheric grid and used the assumption that the ionospheric foot point of
the magnetic ﬁeld is ﬁxed in the ionosphere for the process of magnetic reconﬁguration. Therefore, a measure
of the ﬁeld displacement can be used as a proxy for the inductive component of the electric ﬁeld. However, the
presence of ionospheric convection invalidates the assumption that the magnetic ﬁeld lines can be treated
as frozen in a stationary ionosphere.

2. Calculating the Inductive Electric Field

4. Application: 5 August 2011 Event
4.1. Model Setup
The formalism described aboveis implemented for stormtime,speciﬁcallyfor the5August2011 geomagnetic
storm. This event was simulated using the Space Weather Modeling Framework (SWMF), which integrates
numerical models customized to self-consistently simulate physical processes in the near-Earth space [Tóth
etal., 2005, 2007, 2012]. This numerical framework has been extensively used for scientiﬁc studies of geospace
bymanyauthors[e.g.,Tóthetal.,2005,2007;YuandRidley,2009;Ganushkinaetal.,2010;Ilieetal.,2010a,2010b;
Liemohn et al., 2011; Ilie et al., 2013; Liemohn et al., 2013; Ilie et al., 2015]. The physics domains included in the
simulations are the global magnetosphere (GM), ionosphere electrodynamics (IE), inner magnetosphere, and
polar wind.
The GM component, which couples to all the other modules used here, is represented by the multiﬂuid version
of the Block Adaptive Tree Solar wind-type Roe Upwind Scheme (BATS-R-US) global magnetohydrodynamic
(MHD) model [Powell et al., 1999; Tóth et al., 2006; Glocer et al., 2009a] that solves for the transfer of mass
and energy from the solar wind to the magnetosphere. In the regions close to the Earth, the grid overlaps
with that of the inner magnetosphere component, represented by the Rice Convection Model [Harel et al.,
1981; De Zeeuw et al., 2004]. The ionospheric electrodynamics (IE) component uses the model of Ridley and
Liemohn [2002] and Ridley et al. [2004]. It consists of an electric potential solver and a model of the electron
precipitation which serve to provide the height-integrated ionospheric quantities at an altitude of ∼110 km.
The ionospheric outﬂow of electrons and O+, He+, H+ ions is described by the Polar Wind Outﬂow Model
[Gombosi et al., 1992; Glocer et al., 2009b] and based on the gyrotropic transport equations solved along a
number of magnetic ﬁeld lines [Gombosi and Nagy, 1989; Glocer et al., 2009b; Ilie et al., 2015]. The simulation
setup for this event is described in detail in Ilie et al. [2013, 2015]. The method described in section 3 is applied
in postprocessing mode on a homogeneous grid imposed on the BATS-R-US grid.

5. Summary
We have separated the contribution of the inductive (electrodynamic) component from the potential
(electrostatic) component of the electric ﬁeld by decomposing the motional electric ﬁeld into a superposition
of an irrotational and a solenoidal vector. We found that a localized change in the magnetic ﬁeld generates an
inductive electric ﬁeld whose eﬀect extends over all space, meaning that the eﬀect of the inductive electric
ﬁeld is global even though the changes in the magnetic ﬁeld are localized.
Though computationally expensive, this three-dimensional Biot-Savart-like integration (over the global mag-
netosphere domain) approach removes the need to trace-independent ﬁeld lines and lifts assumptions of
static, nonconvecting ionospheres. However, it assumes that the time-varying magnetic ﬁeld is vanishing
on the boundary. The integration domain can be limited to the region which includes the magnetopause
currents, since contributions from the solar wind outside the magnetopause tend to be negligible by com-
parison. This is due to the fact that the eﬀect of the time-varying magnetic ﬁeld falls oﬀwith inverse of the
square distance.
This study presents evidence that during periods of magnetic disturbance, the electric ﬁeld induced by the
localized change in magnetic ﬁeld can be comparable in certain regions to the convective electric ﬁelds. The
application of this formalism on simulation results from SWMF predicts inductive ﬁelds with magnitude com-
parable with those reported in the literature. In the cases we selected, the magnitude of the inductive ﬁeld
was of the order of several mV/m in regions where the eﬀect of the time-varying magnetic ﬁeld was strongest.
In these regions, the inductive component dominated the potential component of the electric ﬁeld. Further-
more, the inductive ﬁeld exhibits signiﬁcant spatial and temporal variations which means that particles that
drift into diﬀerent regions of space are being exposed to diﬀerent means of acceleration.
To sum up, our results suggest that the inductive electric ﬁeld could have a substantial contribution to parti-
cle energization in the near-Earth region even though the changes in the magnetic ﬁelds occur at distances of
several tens of RE. This ﬁnding is particularly important for ring current modeling which in most cases excludes
inductive contributions to the total particle drift. For instance, it is possible that in localized regions in space
the ⃗EΦ and ⃗EI vectorcomponentspartiallycanceleachother[Amm,1997],leadingtoanear-zerolocalizedelec-
tric ﬁeld. Therefore, ring current models, which only account for the potential component (⃗E = ⃗EΦ), severely
overestimate the electric ﬁeld, and subsequent particles drift in the ring current evolution. Accounting for
the eﬀect of the time-changing ﬁelds in these models would lead to an appropriate description of near-Earth
current systems, which is the key for understanding the dynamics of the inner magnetosphere.
Another application where the separation of the magnetospheric electric ﬁeld into a potential part and
an inductive part would be very useful is in the calculation of radiation belt radial diﬀusion coeﬃcients
[e.g., Fei et al., 2006, and references therein]. Currently, even though the “electric” radial diﬀusion coeﬃcient
is typically assumed to arise from potential electric ﬁelds, the total electric ﬁeld is used instead. Also, any
inductive electric ﬁeld contributions are assumed to be contained in the “magnetic” diﬀusion coeﬃcient,
and since the electric and magnetic diﬀusion coeﬃcients are usually simply summed together, the diﬀusive
eﬀects of the inductive electric ﬁeld are probably being incorrectly calculated, by some unknown amount.
With a method of separating the electric ﬁeld, such as in this paper, the eﬀect of the potential versus inductive
electric ﬁelds can be better understood and quantiﬁed. This is a topic of future work.
