Ion acceleration at dipolarization fronts
in the inner magnetosphere
A. Y. Ukhorskiy1, M. I. Sitnov1
, V. G. Merkin1
, M. Gkioulidou1

1Applied Physics Laboratory, The Johns Hopkins University, Laurel, Maryland, USA
Abstract During geomagnetic storms plasma pressure in the inner magnetosphere is controlled by
energetic ions of tens to hundreds of keV. Plasma pressure is the source of global storm time currents,
which control the distribution of magnetic ﬁeld and couple the inner magnetosphere and the ionosphere.
Recent analysis showed that the buildup of hot ion population in the inner magnetosphere largely occurs
in the form of localized discrete injections associated with sharp dipolarizations of magnetic ﬁeld, similar to
dipolarization fronts in the magnetotail. Because of signiﬁcant diﬀerences between the ambient magnetic
ﬁeld and the dipolarization front properties in the magnetotail and the inner magnetosphere, the physical
mechanisms of ion acceleration at dipolarization fronts in these two regions may also be diﬀerent. In this
paper we discuss a new acceleration mechanism enabled by stable trapping of ions at the azimuthally
localized dipolarization fronts. It is shown that trapping can provide a robust mechanism of ion energization
in the inner magnetosphere even in the absence of large electric ﬁelds.
1. Introduction
During geomagnetic storms a large volume of ions is injected from the magnetotail into the inner magne-
tosphere. Energized ions become the dominant source of plasma pressure in the inner magnetosphere. Hot
plasma pressure drives large electrical currents which determine global electrodynamics and coupling within
the inner magnetosphere-ionosphere system [e.g., Vasyliunas, 1984; Roelof et al., 2004]. Pressure-driven cur-
rents determine the distribution of magnetic ﬁeld across the inner magnetosphere. Time evolution of the
ﬁeld determines the motion of radiation belt particles and can be responsible for rapid electron losses during
storm main phase [e.g., Ukhorskiy et al., 2006; Bortnik et al., 2006; Kim et al., 2008; Turner et al., 2014; Ukhorskiy
et al., 2015]. Hot plasma also provides the energy source for multiple instabilities generating wave modes
that produce local acceleration and loss of radiation belt electrons (see reviews for Friedel et al. [2002], Hudson
et al. [2008], and Thorne [2010]), drive their radial transport [e.g., Lanzerotti et al., 1969; Ukhorskiy et al., 2009],
and cause precipitation of energetic particles into the ionosphere (see review for Millan and Thorne [2007]).
Understanding the buildup of hot plasma pressure produced by transport and acceleration of ions is therefore
essential for resolving the physics of the inner magnetosphere and the magnetosphere-ionosphere coupling.
Transport and energization of ions in the magnetotail occur largely in the form of discrete activations asso-
ciated with sharp and strong dipolarizations of the magnetic ﬁeld (dipolarization fronts) [e.g., Nakamura
et al., 2002; Runov et al., 2009; Angelopoulos et al., 2013] and fast plasma bulk ﬂows (bursty bulk ﬂows)
[e.g., Baumjohann et al., 1990; Angelopoulos et al., 1992; Shiokawa et al., 1997; Fairﬁeld et al., 1999]. According
to statistical analysis of the in situ measurements in the magnetotail, an increase in the Bz component at
dipolarization fronts varies between several nanoteslas to tens of nanoteslas [e.g., Ohtani et al., 2004; Runov
et al., 2009, 2015], while the typical earthward speed of plasma ﬂows varies between 300 and 600 km/s
[e.g., Angelopoulos et al., 1992; Ohtani et al., 2004]. From multipoint measurements [Nakamura et al., 2004] it
also follows that the magnetotail plasma ﬂows are localized to 2–3 RE in the azimuthal direction and have a
narrow extent of 1.5–2 RE around the magnetic equator. Owing to the characteristic scale size of ∼1 RE, par-
ticle acceleration events associated with such localized ﬁeld and/or ﬂow structures are often referred to as
mesoscale injections [e.g., Sergeev et al., 1996].
Whileisolatedimpulsivedipolarizationeventswereobserveddeepinsidetheinnermagnetosphere[e.g.,Nosé
et al., 2010], earlier statistical studies [e.g., Ohtani et al., 2006; Dubyagin et al., 2011; Sergeev et al., 2012]
reported that only a small fraction of dipolarizations can propagate inside geosynchronous orbit suggest-
ing that mesoscale injections do not substantially contribute to ion transport in the inner magnetosphere.

A new perspective on the importance of the mesoscale transport in the inner magnetosphere emerged
from the analysis of the data from the Van Allen Probes mission [Mauk et al., 2012]. With two spacecraft
in near-identical low-inclination (10∘) elliptical orbits with low apogees of 5.8 RE, and the period of about
9 h, the Probes provided a factor of 10 increase in the sampling rate of the equatorial inner magnetosphere
compared to previous missions. Gkioulidou et al. [2014] analyzed Van Allen Probes energetic ion data during
17 March 2013 geomagnetic storm. They showed that multiple mesoscale injections were observed through-
out the storm main phase deep inside the inner magnetosphere and that these injections accounted for a
large portion of plasma pressure in the storm time ring current. Gkioulidou et al. [2015] reported a disper-
sionless ion injections at as low as L = 5.5 associated with a sharp dipolarization of magnetic ﬁeld, similar to
dipolarization fronts in the magnetotail. A statistical analysis of dipolarization fronts measured at Van Allen
Probes [Liu et al., 2016] showed that as much as 30% of dipolarizations from the magnetotail can penetrate
inside geosynchronous orbit.
Over the years a number of physical mechanisms were put forward to explain ion energization at dipolariza-
tion fronts in the magnetotail. In a series of publications, BirnandHesse [1994] and Birnetal. [1997, 2000, 2012,
2015] showed, on the basis of test particle tracing in MHD ﬁelds, that fast earthward ﬂows in the magnetotail
can produce large (>10 mV/m) electric ﬁelds that can accelerate ions by an order of magnitude over a single
ion interaction with the front, i.e., over the portion of the dawn-dusk ion motion traversing through the elec-
tric ﬁeld pulse. Zhouetal. [2010, 2011] pointed out that in the magnetotail the magnetic ﬁeld amplitude ahead
of a propagating front can be much smaller than the ﬁeld amplitude behind the front. Thus, the azimuthal
electric ﬁeld behind the front can be substantially higher than the background convection. Consequently,
ions can be energized by reﬂection from the fronts, similar to particle reﬂection from quasi-perpendicular
shocks [e.g., Terasawa, 1979; Gosling et al., 1982]. Artemyev et al. [2012] and then Ukhorskiy et al. [2013] con-
sidered implications of negative magnetic ﬁeld depletions ahead of dipolarization fronts. In this case the ions
can be stably trapped at the reconnection point, formed ahead of the front, and accelerated by the electric
ﬁeld associated with the front motion, similar to the surfatron acceleration [e.g., Sagdeev, 1966; Katsouleasand
Dawson, 1983].
Given signiﬁcant diﬀerences between the ambient magnetic ﬁeld and dipolarization front properties in the
magnetotail and the inner magnetosphere, it is unlikely that the same acceleration mechanisms can oper-
ate in both regions. Both the reﬂection and the surfatron acceleration mechanisms require that the magnetic
ﬁeld be much smaller ahead of the dipolarization than behind, which clearly is not the case in the inner mag-
netosphere. In the inner magnetosphere ion acceleration by the electric ﬁeld pulses is limited by the time
it takes ions to traverse the azimuthal extent of the pulse, which is controlled by the magnitude of the gra-
dient and curvature drift. Recent simulations [Artemyev et al., 2015] showed that intense electric ﬁeld pulses
15–20 mV/m can eﬀectively accelerate ions (i.e., factor of 10 increase in the phase space density at 100 keV)
outside geosynchronous orbit L ∼7–9. Similar acceleration deep inside geosynchronous orbit may require
even larger electric ﬁelds. According to a recent statistical study [Liu et al., 2016], the electric ﬁelds associ-
ated with mesoscale injections inside geosynchronous can be as large as ≳10 mV/m, but on average do not
exceed 6 mV/m.
In this paper we discuss a new mechanism of ion acceleration at dipolarization fronts in the inner magneto-
sphere that does not require strong electric ﬁelds; the acceleration is enabled by ion trapping at the inverse
magnetic ﬁeld gradient, which forms at the interface between the front and the background plasma. Our
analysis is based on three-dimensional test particle simulations of ion motion in the electromagnetic ﬁeld of
earthward propagating dipolarization fronts. The following section describes the analytical model of dipo-
larization fronts used in the simulations. The physical mechanism of ion trapping at the front is discussed in
section 3. Sections 4, 5, and 6 followed by conclusions present the results of our numerical simulations.

3. Stable Trapping
One mechanism that can enable large particle acceleration even in the absence of a strong electric ﬁeld is
trapping. In the case of an azimuthally localized dipolarization front, trapping is produced by inverse mag-
netic gradient at the interface between the front and the ambient plasma. In the reference frame moving with
the front, the guiding center of a trapped particle circles the front. Since the ambient magnetic ﬁeld increases
as the front propagates earthward, the magnitude of the magnetic ﬂux through the guiding center orbit also
grows. This produces an inductive electric ﬁeld that accelerates the particle. Since a trapped particle circles
the front, a potential electric ﬁeld does not produce net variations in particle energy. The eﬀect resembles
acceleration of radiation belt particles due to global increases in magnetic ﬂux across their longitudinal drift
orbits around Earth, with the following diﬀerence. In the case of radiation belt particle, the spatial and tem-
poral scales of the longitudinal drift are decoupled from the scales of the gyromotion by multiple orders of
magnitude. Consequently, global variations of the ﬁeld generally do not violate the ﬁrst invariant of radia-
tion belt particles. In the case of the discussed mechanism, on the other hand, the trapped motion and the
gyromotion are not as strongly decoupled, which may lead to the violation of the magnetic moment.

4. Three-Dimensional Test Particle Simulations
For a quantitative analysis we conducted three-dimensional test particle simulation of energetic proton inter-
action with an isolated earthward propagating dipolarization front as discussed in Gkioulidou et al. [2015].
Particle trajectories in the prescribed ﬁelds given by the superposition of the ambient ﬁeld (Earth’s dipole
with a disk-shaped current sheet) and the dipolarization front were computed numerically by integrating the
Lorentz equation with a symplectic adaptive stepsize Boris algorithm [e.g., BirdsallandLangdon, 2014]. A dipo-
larization front with parameters described in Appendix B and the azimuthal parameters a𝜑= 9 and Δ𝜑= 0.1
(same as in the simpliﬁed consideration in the previous section) was launched at L = 7.5 (equatorial loca-
tion of ETS-8) at midnight and propagated earthward to L= 5.5 (the location of Probe B) at constant velocity
of ur = −140 km/s. Consequently, the electric ﬁeld of the front in this simulations did not have an inertial
term (second term on the right-hand side of equation (4). The eﬀects of varying front speed are discussed in
section 6.
The simulation consisted of 20 parallel runs (2.56 ⋅107 test particles in total). In each run we used a random
number generator to evenly distribute test particle initial conditions in each of the ﬁve dimensions of the
phase space: (L, 𝜑, K, 𝛼eq, and 𝜓), where 𝜓is the gyrophase and 𝛼eq is the equatorial pitch angle (see Table 1).
Particles were initialized in the equatorial plane along the propagation path of the front. To minimize the
number of particles in the simulations, the particles were initiated (i.e., set in motion) only when they were in
close proximity to the front (ΔL < 0.15).

5. The Eﬀects of Mass and Charge State
The analysis of dispersed ion injections measured by RBSPICE shows that He++ ions are accelerated to higher,
approximately factor of 2, energies than H+ and He+, which exhibit similar acceleration. This suggests that ion
acceleration at dipolarization fronts depends on the ion mass and the charge state. To investigate the mass
and charge eﬀects on trapping and acceleration, werepeated the simulation described in the previous section
for H+, He+, and He++ ions. Due to computational feasibility considerations the simulations were restricted
to the equatorially mirroring particles and a singular value of the ion initial energy of 50 keV, which yielded a
factor of 320 reduction of the number of test particles (see Table 1).
The simulation results are summarized in Figure 6. Figures 6a–6c show the snapshots of the test particle loca-
tions in the equatorial plane at the end of the simulations (when the front propagated down to L = 5.5) using
the same format as Figures 4a–4c. In all three cases there are distinct populations of ions trapped behind the
front and accelerated above 100 keV with a few noticeable diﬀerences. He++ ions are accelerated to higher
energies than H+ and He+ ions. H+ and He++ ions accelerated to the energies above 200 keV are concentrated
along the eastward edge of the trap, while He+ ions appear to be evenly distributed over the trap area.
Figures 6d–6f show energy distributions of ions at the end of the simulations. From the comparisons of the

greater than 200 keV population are accelerated above 250 keV; there are no H+ or He+ ions above 250 keV.

7. Conclusions
Inthisstudyweanalyzedionaccelerationatdipolarizationfrontsintheinnermagnetosphere.Forthispurpose
we used three-dimensional test particle simulations of ion motion at an earthward propagating dipolarization
front. Three-dimensional electromagnetic ﬁeld of the front was described with an analytical model with the
parameters constrained by observations. To control the stretching of the ambient magnetic ﬁeld, we used a
current disk model.
The main conclusion of the analysis is that azimuthally localized dipolarization fronts can provide a robust
mechanism of ion acceleration in theinner magnetosphereeven in theabsenceof largeelectric ﬁelds. Ionsget
stably trapped at inverse magnetic ﬁeld gradient at the interface between the front and the ambient plasma.
Trapping enables persistent interaction of ions with electric ﬁeld induced by the front motion producing large
acceleration: the intensities of 100–300 keV ions increase by a factor of 10, which is in good agreement with
in situ observations. This result is stable, i.e., is not sensitive to variations in the parameters of the front such
as the azimuthal width, the sharpness of the gradient, and the inward propagation speed.

It is important to discuss model limitations and the subsequent inconsistencies with observations, which we
plan on addressing in future publications. First, our analytical model does not capture realistic ﬁeld-aligned
distribution of dipolarization fronts. A dipolarization in the model is localized in the z direction in the vicin-
ity of the equatorial plane. Consequently, the magnetic trap has a loss cone; only particles with large pitch
angles can be trapped at the front. As a result energized ions have a pancake pitch angle distribution as
opposed to isotropic distributions reported from the observations [Gkioulidou et al., 2014]. Second, as was
previously mentioned, observations of ion injections suggest that He++ ions are accelerated to the energies
approximately twice larger than H+ and He+ ions, whereas in our simulation the diﬀerence in acceleration
is not as large. Third, similar to previous studies [e.g., Artemyev et al., 2012; Greco et al., 2014], the magnetic
perturbation of the front was approximated with a soliton-like quasi-stationary structure propagating earth-
ward relative to global magnetospheric convection. A fully self-consistent model of dipolarization fronts in
the inner magnetosphere is required to validate this assumption.
