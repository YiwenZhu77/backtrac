Energetic particle loss through the magnetopause:
A combined global MHD and test-particle study
K. A. Sorathia1
, V. G. Merkin1
, A. Y. Ukhorskiy1, B. H. Mauk1
, and D. G. Sibeck2
1The Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory, Laurel, Maryland, USA, 2NASA Goddard Space Flight Center,
Greenbelt, Maryland, USA
Abstract We study the spatiotemporal characteristics of energetic particle losses from the
magnetosphere using test-particle trajectories in electromagnetic ﬁelds from a global magnetosphere
magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) simulation. We use a dynamically evolving distribution of high-resolution
electromagnetic ﬁelds from the Lyon-Fedder-Mobarry global MHD model and trace large ensembles of
100 keV hydrogen and oxygen ions as well as electrons from a near-Earth plasma sheet location through
their escape from the magnetosphere. In agreement with recent MMS observations, we demonstrate that
both ions and electrons have access to and escape throughout the dayside magnetopause, including
magnetically drift-shadowed regions. Also, in agreement with MMS observations, the depth of penetration
and persistence of particles in the magnetosheath has a clear mass dependence, heavier particles
penetrating further and lingering longer. We demonstrate both magnetic local time and latitude
dependence of particles losses as manifested by their crossings of the open-closed boundary and
relate them to the complex ﬁeld topology. Finally, we establish a signiﬁcant role of Kelvin-Helmholtz
instability in facilitating particle losses at the magnetopause ﬂanks.
1. Introduction
Energetic particle (greater than or equivalent to tens of keV) escape from the magnetosphere has received
renewed interested recently due to in situ observations made by the Magnetospheric Multiscale (MMS) mis-
sion at the dayside magnetopause [Cohen et al., 2016; Westlake et al., 2016]. Historically, observations of
magnetospheric ions and electrons in the magnetosheath have been associated with either ﬁnite Larmor
radius eﬀects [e.g., Sibeck et al., 1987; Kudela et al., 1992] or magnetic reconnection [e.g., Korth et al., 1982;
Mitchell et al., 1987], the former mechanism favored due to poor correlation of particle escape with conditions
required for reconnection [Sibeck et al., 1987]. More recent extensions of the former mechanism involving
particles leaking through the magnetopause due to an outward magnetic gradient drift inside the magne-
tosphere have also been reported [Keika et al., 2005; Kim et al., 2005]. Statistical studies show that energetic
ions can be observed both on the dawnside and duskside of the magnetosphere, although with signiﬁcant
local time asymmetry in their spectra, also advocating for the ﬁnite Larmor radius model [Paschalidis et al.,
1994]. The model posits that energetic ions access the duskside magnetopause preferentially as they drift
westward from their source region in the near-Earth magnetotail and similarly eastward drifting electrons
will preferentially access the dawnside magnetopause. However, even in a simpliﬁed static model of the mag-
netic and electric ﬁelds, guiding center drifts of energetic ions can bring them to the dawnside magnetopause
[Paschalidis et al., 1994] due to an accumulated E × B drift. Although not the focus of this study, energetic
ions of magnetospheric origin have also been observed upstream of the bow shock [Kronberg et al., 2011, and
references therein].
Recently, Cohen et al. [2016] presented MMS observations of both electrons and ions (H+ and Hen+) as the
spacecraft crossed the magnetopause in the postnoon sector. The presence of electrons in the dayside dusk
magnetosheath conﬁrmed that their trajectories inside the magnetosphere are not necessarily prohibited
from accessing the dusk magnetopause, which is at variance with the expectation from a static picture of mag-
netopause drift shadowing [e.g., Sibeck et al., 1987] in which charged particle losses exhibit strong dawn-dusk
bias based on the quadrant in which their drift shell ﬁrst encounters the magnetopause. The observations
showed depletion of particles (both electrons and light ions) with ﬁeld-aligned pitch angles on both the mag-
netosheath and the magnetosphere sides, which was interpreted as particles escaping via reconnected ﬁeld
lines with a component of the magnetic ﬁeld normal to the magnetopause. At the same time, the model

signatures across the diﬀerent particle populations, including electrons, light, and heavy ions, during the
MMS magnetopause crossing. Still, the model made a number of simplifying assumptions, motivating a study
into the details of energetic particle escape that can be gleaned from a model with more complex electro-
magnetic ﬁeld conﬁgurations. This paper reports on such an investigation. In particular, we consider herein a
dynamically changing and spatially structured ﬁeld environment taken from a high-resolution global mange-
tohydrodynamic (MHD) simulation of the magnetosphere. The speciﬁc salient simpliﬁcations of the Mauketal.
[2016] model that are relaxed here are as follows: the assumption of only azimuthal gradients of the magnetic
ﬁeld, uniform distribution of the ﬁelds in the north-south direction, a spatially uniform magnetosheathplasma
ﬂow, and a static smooth magnetopause boundary. As discussed below, the magnetospheric boundary
in the global MHD simulation is highly dynamic, exhibiting, in particular, strong Kelvin-Helmholtz (KH) oscil-
lations [Merkin et al., 2013] with clear nonlinear signatures at the ﬂanks. The particular questions that we
attempt to answer in this paper are as follows: How common are energetic particle losses in drift-shadowed
regions? What is the particle dynamics in the vicinity of the magnetopause before and after their transition
from closed to open ﬁeld lines? What is the spatial distribution of the losses? What is the role of the KH insta-
bility, energy-dependent drifts, bulk plasma ﬂows, and magnetic topology? This paper does not intend to
give an all-encompassing answer to these questions but hopefully sheds some new light on these signiﬁcant
problems.
2. Methodology
Our technical approach is built upon a combination of two simulation methods, whereby the dynamics
of energetic particles are studied by tracing them through electromagnetic ﬁelds obtained from a high-
resolution global MHD simulation of the magnetosphere. For the ion species, H+ and O+, we calculate the
trajectories using the full Lorentz equations. These equations are solved using a relativistic Boris algorithm
[Birdsall and Langdon, 2005, p. 356] with an adaptive time step, Δt = 𝜀p∕F, where p is the particle momentum,
F is the Lorentz force and 𝜀is a preselected small number. Electrons are evolved using a hybrid method,
utilizing the Hamiltonian formulation of the guiding center equations [Ukhorskiy and Sitnov, 2013] when pos-
sible and switching to the full Lorentz equations when the local ﬁeld conditions render the guiding center
approximation invalid. The condition for this switch is 𝜌g|∇B|∕B > 10−3, where 𝜌g is the particle gyroradius and
B∕|∇B| deﬁnes the characteristic length scale of the magnetic ﬁeld gradient. The guiding center trajectory is
transformed to the full Lorentz trajectory using a randomly generated gyrophase angle.
The global magnetosphere simulation was performed using the Lyon-Fedder-Mobarry (LFM) MHD code
[Lyon et al., 2004]. The electromagnetic ﬁelds used to obtain the results presented in this work were taken
from the simulation run published earlier by Wiltberger et al. [2015], who studied fast ﬂow channels in the
magnetotail. We include here the salient details, while the full description of the MHD simulation can be found

3. Results
3.1. Overview of the Simulation
An overview of the simulation is shown in Figure 2. It displays a series of still images depicting the evolution
of the z component of the residual magnetic ﬁeld, 𝛿Bz, in the equatorial plane. Active particles, i.e., those
remaining in the simulation domain, are marked at the location of their last crossing of the equatorial plane
and colored by energy. The panels in Figure 2 are taken from Movie S4 that is included in the supporting
information (SI).
The particles initialized on the nightside follow their drift orbits until making the ﬁrst contact with the magne-
topause at T ≈400 s, which is approximately the same for all species since they have the same initial energy.
Although all the test particles are initialized with the same initial energy, there is an appreciable spread in
energies prior to ﬁrst contact with the magnetopause. Upon contact with the magnetopause we ﬁrst begin
to see appreciable particle losses into the magnetosheath. In the ﬁrst and second columns of Figure 2 we see
variation in the persistence of particles in the magnetosheath as the ensemble of particles drifts toward noon.
The marked positions of the last equatorial crossing for the electrons closely match the magnetospheric
boundary as delineated by the magnetic ﬁeld. This means that electrons crossing the magnetopause do not
cross the equatorial plane in the magnetosheath, i.e., they are lost at least as fast as the time cadence of the
ﬁeld topology mapping (0.1 s) allows to determine. The marked positions of ions do not align with the mag-
netopause boundary to the same extent. In particular, we ﬁnd that O+ exhibits numerous, repeated equatorial
crossings deep into the magnetosheath, a point we expect to have observational consequences and return
to in section 3.2.
The third column of Figure 2 shows the particle populations crossing noon and entering the second part of
their drift orbits, dawnward for ions, and duskward for electrons. It is in this region that the consequences
of drift shadowing would be expected to manifest as a suppressed magnetopause loss rate, although we
will see that this is not the case. We note the diﬀerence in the spatial distribution of the O+, and less evi-
dently H+, ions relative to the magnetopause at noon as compared to dusk. The penetration of ions into the
magnetosheath here is signiﬁcantly reduced. In the last column, we see the behavior of the particles as they
encounter well-formed KH vortices, at magnetic local time (MLT) of approximately 0800 (ions) and 1600 (e−),
after moving past the comparatively smooth magnetopause surface around noon. We note that both the ions
and the electrons appear to have access to the magnetopause ﬂanks during the second part of their drift
orbits, a combined eﬀect of a smooth sunward E × B drift near noon and dynamic bulk ﬂows near the ﬂanks.
Figure 3 complements Figure 2 by concentrating speciﬁcally on ion equatorial crossings in a more statistical
manner. The ﬁgure shows the density of equatorial crossings for particles on open ﬁeld lines. Note that there
is an implicit bias in the ﬁgure because more particles are lost at dusk than at dawn simply because they
reach there ﬁrst. Regardless of this bias, Figure 3 makes a number of important points. First, the probability
of observing O+ deeper in the magnetosheath is higher at dusk than at dawn. This is because of the energy
ﬁlter eﬀect, whereby the higher energy, larger gyroradius particles are preferentially lost at dusk [Paschalidis
et al., 1994] (see also Figure S1 included in the supporting information.) Second, while the energy ﬁlter eﬀect
operates for both ion species, the asymmetry in the depth of magnetosheath penetration is much more pro-
nounced for O+ rather than the H+ ions, suggesting that ﬁnite Larmor radius eﬀects are more important for
the O+ loss mechanism than for H+. Third, aside from the depth of the penetration, the MLT dependence of the
H+ losses is far more dawn-dusk symmetric than that of O+ ions, e.g., the diﬀerence in probabilities of observ-
ing H+ at the dusk versus dawn terminator is smaller than the same diﬀerence for O+. Finally, there are clear
bumps in the equatorial crossings at 0800 and 1600 MLT, most evident in the H+ distribution. We believe that
these are caused by the Kelvin-Helmholtz instability (KHI) activity. Before moving on we note that even with
the greater magnetosheath penetration of O+ ions, we do not observe for the parameters simulated here any
appreciable presence of ions upstream of the bow shock in the near-equatorial region [Kronberg et al., 2011].

4. Discussion and Conclusions
In this paper we investigated energetic particle (H+, O+, and e−) loss through the magnetopause boundary
using a test-particle approach with a high-resolution global magnetospheric MHD model. We have initiated
an ensemble of 100 keV particles of each species in the nightside magnetosphere and studied, in a quanti-
tative and statistical fashion, their losses through the dynamic magnetospheric boundary. Our results can be
summarized as follows.
In agreement with recent MMS observations [Cohen et al., 2016], energetic particles originating inside the
magnetosphere can be lost on either side (postnoon or prenoon) of the dayside magnetosphere. In our sim-
ulations we ﬁnd that while ions (electrons) escape preferentially at dusk (dawn) because their trajectories
reach those regions ﬁrst as they drift around the earth, the losses are still ubiquitous throughout the dayside.
In addition, in our fully dynamic 3-D electromagnetic ﬁelds we ﬁnd at least two signiﬁcant factors that can
bring particles to the magnetospheric boundary where they eventually get lost. Figure 12 illustrates both of
these factors. The ﬁgure shows, using equatorial electromagnetic ﬁeld data at a ﬁducial time, the energy at
which the magnitude of the magnetic gradient drift would equal that of the E × B drift. Two points are clear.
First, near the equatorial ﬂanks of the magnetosphere even ∼100 keV particles can be picked up by the bulk
ﬂow and be entrained in the vortical motions associated with the KHI activity at the low-latitude boundary
layer. The electric ﬁeld strength in these near-vortex regions peaks at approximately 10 mV/m. Second, the
sunward E × B drift in the quiet subsolar region, with characteristic electric ﬁeld strengths of 1 mV/m, is suf-
ﬁciently strong that ∼100 keV particles will feel an additional nudge toward the magnetopause there as they
drift around. We that believe these two factors play a signiﬁcant role in bringing the energetic particles toward
the magnetopause from inside the magnetosphere and thus facilitate their eventual escape, regardless of the
side of the magnetosphere.
Furthermore, we studied the details of the particle losses both in space and time, as well as their persistence
in the magnetosheath, for the diﬀerent species and found these results to also be in agreement with recent

A number of caveats should be noted with regard to our simulations. In this paper our focus has been on
particles of the same initial energy originating in a limited region in the near-Earth tail. Tracing more realistic
initial particle distributions will be necessary to evaluate the statistical properties and mechanisms of their
losses that may operate during observed events. Our choice of electromagnetic ﬁelds has also been limited to
the speciﬁc southward IMF driven conﬁguration. Both the state of the magnetosphere and the distribution of
KH waves at the magnetopause will be diﬀerent during other circumstances and may need to be addressed
on a case by case basis.
Despite the idealized conﬁguration of our MHD simulation, it is encouraging that our test-particle simulations
were able to reproduce a number of key observed features of energetic particle losses as discussed above.
Our results also suggest that magnetopause dynamics may play an important role in particle loss processes.
It is our hope that further numerical investigations in concert with corresponding observations will further
advance the study of this compelling topic.
